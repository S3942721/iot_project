The time-frequency resources in the NB-IoT are limited. For the In-
band mode, the NB-IoT operates in the resources of LTE/SG-NR, and
hence, the time-frequency resources are valuable. The NB-IoT devices
require long-lasting battery power, and hence, the devices should con-
sume less power. Thus, resource utilization and power consumption of
the devices are the key performance indicators of the NB-IoT system.
The set of subframes constituting NPDCCH and NPDSCH is broadcast
to the devices by the base station and is called an NPDCCH period
(NP). As per 3GPP specifications [3], if the base station schedules DCI
of an NB-IoT device in NPDCCH of an NP, then the NPDSCH of the
same NP should carry the data for the corresponding device. Hence, to
reduce the resource wastage, there is a need for allocating resources to
the devices by simultaneously considering both NPDCCH and NPDSCH
requirements. Further, when the base station broadcasts the NP, all the
active NB-IoT devices in the system try to decode the NP. As per 3GPP
constraints [3], an NP can accommodate a maximum of eight NB-IoT
devices. Thus, more the number of devices scheduled in an NP, less
is the overall power consumption of the devices. The joint scheduling
of NB-IoT devices in NPDCCH and NPDSCH, considering the 3GPP
constraints is a challenging problem and can improve the resource
utilization and power consumption of the devices. In the direction,
the joint scheduling problem for NB-IoT has been considered in [6].
However, the adaptation of repetition allocation for the NB-IoT devices
has not been considered in [6]. Further, the essential aspect of receiver
design is also missing in [6]. Motivated by this, the main contributions
of this work (as an extension of [6]) are as follows.

1. We propose an adaptive repetition allocation algorithm and
present a receiver design for the downlink in NB-IoT.

2. We define performance metrics to evaluate the proposed sched-
uler and present the bounds on the performance gains with the
proposed scheduler.

3. We perform system-level and link-level simulations, and show
that the proposed algorithm significantly outperforms the state-
of-the-art algorithms.

The rest of the paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, we
present the related work in the literature. Section 3 describes the
scheduling procedure in NB-IoT as per 3GPP constraints. In Section 4,
we define performance metrics, propose the joint scheduling algorithm,
repetition algorithm, and receiver design. In Section 5, we present the
simulation results. Section 6 discusses some concluding remarks and
possible future works.

2. Related work

In [7], joint resource and power allocation for control channel of
3GPP-LTE has been discussed. The number of resources to be allocated
for a user is varied with the power allocated to that user. This way,
irrespective of the user density, the power and resource optimization
is achieved at the base station. Further, in [7,8], simple sorting based
algorithms have been presented for scheduling the users in the control
channel of LTE. In [9,10], authors have presented a novel rate matching
scheme for the downlink control channel, which reduces the power
consumption of the devices in LTE-M1 technology. In [11], optimal
resource allocation of the control channel has been performed for
3GPP-LTE. To achieve this, authors have formulated resource alloca-
tion as a set-packing problem. With Monte-Carlo simulations, authors
have shown that the optimization significantly increases the system’s
cell-edge performance. In [12], various scheduling algorithms have
been discussed in the context of LTE. The authors have considered
guaranteed bit rate, constraints from random access-procedure, and
real-time traffic models while evaluating the proposed scheduler de-
signs. Through system-level simulation results, authors have shown that
the proposed algorithms improve the QoS for real-time traffic. In [13],
various scheduling procedures and respective trade-offs between en-
ergy efficiency, spectral efficiency, and fairness have been analysed for

the cloud radio access network architectures. In [14], authors have pre-
sented low complexity scheduling algorithms for OFDMA systems with
guaranteed fairness. In [15], authors have proposed a new remapping
strategy based on a linear transformation matrix for the LTE PDCCH.
Through simulation results, authors have shown that the proposed
scheme has better resource utilization and less probability of blocking.
In [16], authors have presented a scheduling algorithm based on the
radio network temporary identifier of the user. Further, authors have
shown that the system performance improves significantly in terms of
resource utilization, allocation delay time, and probability of blocking.
However, all these algorithms are in the context of 3GPP-LTE, and
cannot be directly applied to NB-IoT.

In [17], the constraints and the scheduling process for the downlink
physical layer of 3GPP NB-IoT have been discussed. The problem of
delay assignment for the optimal UE scheduling has been explained
and some heuristic solutions have been provided. However, the issue of
deciding the length of NP has not been addressed. In [18], an optimal
scheduling algorithm for the NPDSCH has been proposed. However,
the authors have assumed that the information of the NPDCCH region
and the NB-IoT devices to be scheduled in each NPDCCH region are
available a priori. The problem of defining the NPDCCH region has not
been addressed in [18]. In [19], an optimal control channel scheduling
algorithm has been presented for NPDCCH. The scheduling problem
of the control channel is formulated with 3GPP constraints and is
optimized for better resource utilization and power consumption of
the NB-IoT devices. However, none of these existing works address
the issue of joint scheduling of both control and data channel regions.
[6] is the first attempt to consider the resource requirements of each
NB-IoT device in both control and shared channel while scheduling
the devices. Further, in this paper, we calculate the optimal length
of the NP, and then, solve an optimization to schedule the devices
in NPDCCH and NPDSCH of the NP. For performance comparison, we
have implemented the scheduling algorithms presented in [19]. Next,
we present the scheduling in NB-IoT and the constraints as per 3GPP
specifications. Further, for ease of reading, a list of acronyms used in

3. Scheduling in NB-IoT

Whenever an NB-IoT device is switched on, it initially performs
the cell search and synchronization procedure to get connected to
the base station. For this synchronization, the device uses NB-loT
primary and secondary synchronization signals termed as NPSS and
NSSS, respectively. As shown in Fig. 1, the base station transmits
NPSS in every 6th subframe of each radio frame and NSSS in 10th
subframe of every alternate radio frame. In case the NB-loT device
is successful in the synchronization procedure, it starts decoding the
NPBCH. The base station transmits NPBCH on SF 0 of every radio
frame, as shown in Fig. 1. Then, for the NB-IoT device to communicate
with the base station or vice-versa, the device needs to know the
region over which it can transmit or receive the data. For this purpose,
the base station transmits DCI in the NPDCCH. The DCI carries all
the information required by the NB-IoT device to decode the data in
NPDSCH. It carries the information about the exact location of the
downlink grants, modulation and coding scheme, and downlink data
duration. All the subframes excluding the NPSS, NSSS, and NPBCH are
available for NPDCCH/NPDSCH, as shown in Fig. 1. However, the base
station conveys the duration of NPDCCH and NPDSCH to the NB-IoT
devices. The base station announces only the NPDCCH region, and thus,
the exact location of the DCI is not known a priori to the NB-IoT device.
In the NPDCCH region, the NB-IoT device performs a blind search for
the DCI. However, 3GPP constraints limit the number of such blind
decodings. In case the DCI is successfully decoded, the NB-IoT device
then decodes the actual data intended for it in the NPDSCH. Next, we
explain the scheduling of the devices in NPDCCH in detail.

3.1. NPDCCH scheduling

In NB-IoT, a resource element (RE) is the smallest time-frequency
resource consisting of one subcarrier during one symbol. A physical
resource block (PRB) comprises of 14 OFDM symbols over 12 sub-
carriers and consists of 168 REs. The NB-loT operates with 180 kHz
of transmission bandwidth, and thus, has one PRB in 1 ms. The REs
available in one PRB form two control channel elements (CCEs). In
NPDCCH, the DCls of the NB-IoT devices get scheduled in units of
CCEs [1]. Further, to ensure the successful reception to a device in the
poor coverage, the scheduled DCI may repeat over (R) subframes, The
number of CCEs and R for each device are decided based on the channel
conditions of the NB-IoT device. When the base station announces the
NPDCCH region over a certain number of subframes (Ryjgx) [3], all the
active NB-IoT devices start decoding the region for the DCI. Since the

after every Rth subframe location in R,,,, subframes. This constraint
is explained with an example as follows. Consider an NPDCCH region
with R,,qx = 4 subframes, and two NB-IoT devices requiring repetitions
of R = 1,2 subframes. Let the base station schedule the device with
R = 1 in the first subframe. Now, the second NB-IoT device which
requires R = 2, cannot be scheduled starting from the second subframe.
As per the 3GPP constraints, the device with R = 2 can be scheduled
only after every 2 subframes. Thus, the DCI for the second NB-loT
device is scheduled from the third subframe, as shown in Fig. 1. These
constraints limit the number of blind decoding attempts that an NB-IoT
device has to perform [19]. However, as explained later in Section 3.2,
in case of improper NPDCCH scheduling, these constraints can result in
a significant resource wastage and power consumption by the NB-IoT
devices.

3.2, NPDSCH scheduling

The base station can schedule the data intended for the NB-loT
device in NPDSCH only after four subframes from the end of its
corresponding DCI in NPDCCH. The NB-IoT devices are assumed to

in [19], to perform the blind decoding in all possible positions, the NB-

4.1. Adaptive repetition allocation algorithm

The base station has information about the operating signal to
interference-plus-noise ratio (SINR) of each NB-IoT device. Motivated
by the Modulation and Coding Scheme (MCS) procedure in LTE and
the resource allocation scheme presented in [10], we propose a novel
method to choose R and N,,, for control and shared channel, re-
spectively. For the allocation of repetitions, we rely on the link-level
simulations carried out for all possible repetition values over a wide-
range of SINR values. The DCIs in NB-IoT are meant for either a
particular NB-IoT device or a group of devices. The DCIs meant for
individual NB-IoT devices are transmitted in UE-specific Search Space
(USS) of the control channel region, whereas, the DCIs meant for a
group of NB-IoT devices are transmitted in Common Search Space
(CSS) of the control channel region. We ially define a reference
SINR as follows. For the DCI transmitted in USS, the reference SINR is
same as the operating SINR of the NB-IoT device, whereas, for the DCI
transmitted in CSS, the reference SINR is the least SINR among all of the
NB-IoT devices. We initially carry-out link-level simulations for each
repetition value and tabulate the block error rate (BLER) for varying
SINR values. Now, for each DCI with a reference SINR, we assign a
repetition value R such that the BLER at the reference SINR is smaller
than f [10]. Typically, p is decided by the industry operator based on
the type of service (for example, # = 0.1 can be used for regular sensor
data, whereas, f = 0.001 can be used for emergency/critical services,
etc.). Similarly, for a payload of Ns, of data in the shared channel, we
choose N,,,, such that the BLER at the reference SINR is smaller than
f. Note that the SINR of a device changes over time because of the
varying channel propagation, interference characteristics, network sce-
narios, etc. This SINR (which includes the impact of channel model and
network scenario) is used for mapping repetition-levels to any device.
Hence, irrespective of the network scenario, the proposed algorithm
ensures that the probability of failed transmissions over time converges
to the chosen threshold (). Thus, the link-performance (BLER) varies
with the selected threshold, but the adaptive nature of the proposed
mechanism is independent of the network scenario. After assigning
repetition values R and N,,, for each NB-IoT device, the scheduler
then decides the devices that are to be allocated with resources in each
subframe. Next, we present the performance metrics and then propose
the scheduler design.

repetition requirements R;. This sorting helps in stacking the devices

In this section, we present a receiver design and explain the blind
decodings of NB-IoT in detail.

Initially, the device will obtain the search space length of the
control channel, Ryng, from RRC messages. Then, the device calculates
all possible configurations of R, as mentioned in (2). For each con-
figuration, the device performs demodulation and descrambling of R
subframes, and soft combines the data present in these R subframes.
The device then performs the other physical layer procedures like de-
rate matching, Viterbi decoding, and CRC check. If the CRC results
in a zero-remainder, then the device assumes the DCI to be valid.
The DCI carries the scheduling information about the transport block
transmitted in the shared channel. Thus, the NB-IoT device obtains the
scheduling related information from DCI and decodes the actual data
transmitted in the shared channel. In case the CRC is failed, then it
increases the repetition value R and performs the decoding procedure
for all possible configurations again. This procedure is repeated until
all possible configurations of R are exhausted or a successful CRC
check. If CRC is failed for all the configurations, then either the DCI
for the device was not transmitted in the search space or the repetition
level transmitted was not sufficient for the device to decode the data.
In both cases, the NB-IoT device has to wait for the next RRC messages.

as mentioned in 4.3. The multiplexed data undergoes further physical

used for the DCI. Hence, it carries out blind decoding for all possible
repetition sizes, which involves de-rate matching, Viterbi decoding, and
cyclic redundancy check (CRC). If the CRC is passed, we consider the
payload to be successfully decoded.

6. Conclusion and future work

In this paper, we have explained the rationale for jointly scheduling
the NB-IoT devices in both control and shared channel. We have
formulated the resource allocation as a set packing problem and pro-
posed a joint scheduling algorithm. We have also proposed an adaptive
repetition allocation algorithm and presented a receiver design for
NB-IoT. Further, we have defined performance metrics to evaluate
the scheduling algorithms in NB-IoT and presented the performance
bounds of the proposed scheduler. We have evaluated and compared
the performance of the proposed scheduler using system-level and
link-level simulations. The simulation results have shown that the
proposed scheduler significantly outperforms the current state-of-the-
art algorithms. With the proposed scheduler, the network operators
can choose a suitable trade-off between resource utilization and power
consumption of the NB-IoT devices. In future, we will implement the
proposed scheduler for an NB-IoT system and evaluate the performance
on hardware testbeds.

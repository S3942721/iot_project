 Abstract:
Low-Power Wide-Area Networks (LPWANs) have become fundamental to the Internet of Things (IoT), with NB-IoT (Narrowband Internet of Things) standing out due to its seamless integration with cellular infrastructure, enhanced coverage, and support for dense deployments. Despite its commercial proliferation, SDR-based physical layer (PHY) exploration for NB-IoT remains limited, particularly in addressing unique complexities such as narrowband signal processing, cellular-specific synchronization sequences, and stringent link budget requirements. This paper bridges this gap by presenting a comprehensive tutorial on SDR-based NB-IoT PHY implementation, focusing on three pillars: robust time-frequency synchronization under severe fading and interference, efficient channel estimation for coherent detection, and experimental performance validation in real-world scenarios. We introduce a first-of-its-kind end-to-end SDR implementation supporting both single-tone and multi-tone transmissions, leveraging commercial off-the-shelf (COTS) platforms. Our novel signal processing workflow achieves synchronization through NPSS-based auto-correlation and NSSS-driven cell-ID detection while incorporating CFO estimation and compensation to mitigate oscillator mismatches. For uplink processing, we detail preamble detection and demodulation, addressing coverage enhancement (CE) levels and adaptive subcarrier spacing configurations. Extensive experiments conducted in both indoor (LOS/NLOS) and outdoor environments demonstrate reliable performance, with Bit Error Rate (BER) and Block Error Rate (BLER) metrics validating resilience under varying repetition counts and propagation conditions. The tutorial offers actionable insights for optimizing PHY-layer design, validated against 3GPP specifications, and lays the foundation for next-generation NB-IoT systems in emerging applications, such as smart cities and industrial automation.

SECTION I.
Introduction

In recent years, Low-Power Wide-Area Networks (LPWANs) have emerged as a cornerstone of the Internet of Things (IoT), enabling cost-effective, long-range connectivity for massive deployments of energy-constrained devices. Driven by applications in smart cities, industrial automation, and environmental monitoring, LPWAN technologies such as LoRa [1], Sigfox [2], and NB-IoT (Narrowband Internet of Things) [3] have witnessed exponential growth. Among these, NB-IoT, standardized by 3GPP [4], [5], stands out for its seamless integration with existing cellular infrastructure, enhanced coverage, and support for dense device deployments.

The physical layer (PHY) design of LPWAN systems is critical to achieving their promised performance, particularly in synchronization robustness [6], channel estimation accuracy [7], and energy efficiency [8]. The establishment of end-to-end SDR-based NB-IoT physical layer communication system enables comprehensive protocol stack validation across all layers, while facilitating empirical validation of congestion control mechanisms, collision resolution algorithms, and AI-enhanced demodulation techniques through fully-instrumented experimental frameworks. While software-defined radio (SDR)-based PHY research has gained traction for its flexibility in prototyping and algorithm validation, existing studies have predominantly focused on non-cellular LPWANs like LoRa. For instance, recent works have demonstrated SDR implementations of LoRa PHY [9], [10], [11], [12], [13], [14], [15], [16], addressing challenges such as time-frequency synchronization and interference mitigation. In contrast, despite NB-IoT’s commercial adoption, its SDR-based PHY exploration remains limited, with few studies [17], [18], [19] tackling its unique complexities, such as narrowband signal processing, cellular-specific synchronization sequences, and stringent link budget requirements. ∙ Robust synchronization under severe fading and interference:

Time-frequency synchronization is a critical step in NB-IoT signal processing, forming the foundation for all subsequent operations. Achieving synchronization solely through the signal itself without relying on higher-layer resource allocation poses significant challenges. Time offset (TO) occurs when the signal arrival time is not accurately estimated. NB-IoT uses Timing Advance (TA) from the Evolved Packet Core (EPC) that describes the signal propagation delay to estimate the signal arrival time [3]. However, without EPC, we need to achieve time synchronization based on the received NB signal. Additionally, sampling clock frequency offset (SFO) arises due to oscillator mismatch between transceivers, leading to errors during fast Fourier transform and causing a notable degradation in communication performance [26]. This issue is particularly pronounced in low-cost SDR platforms with unstable oscillators. We design a signal processing workflow that accomplishes time-frequency synchronization for various signals in NB-IoT [17]. We achieve this by performing downlink (DL) synchronization using synchronization signal and uplink (UL) synchronization based on Cyclic Prefix (CP) and reference signal.

∙ Efficient channel estimation for coherent detection: Radio Frequency (RF) signals will be interfered by the channel environment, multi-path effect, and other factors during transmission [27]. In wireless communication systems, channel estimation plays a pivotal role in achieving reliable signal reception by compensating for multi-path fading and interference effects [28]. For UL and DL, we use different reference signals defined in protocols for channel estimation and channel equalization, respectively.

Take the indoor interior case as an example, the system exhibits robust performance with Bit Error Rates (BER) below 1×10−2 and 0% Block Error Rates (BLER).

SECTION II.
NB-IoT Primer

NB-IoT is an LPWAN technology specifically engineered for low energy consumption, extensive coverage, and massive connectivity. Through power-saving techniques including Power-Saving Modes (PSM) and extended Discontinuous Reception (eDRX) [29], NB-IoT achieves ultra-low power operation with device standby durations extending up to 10 years, while supporting over 50,000 terminal connections per cell [30]. Its network architecture comprises terminal devices, radio access networks, core networks, and IoT platforms, with data transmission schemes optimized through enhancements to the control plane and user plane. During signaling procedures, User Equipment (UE) may initiate non-IP connections through attachment processes without establishing conventional Packet Data Network (PDN) connections, enabling data transfer via Non-Access Stratum (NAS) messages. The operational states, including connected, idle, and PSM modes, are managed through timer-based state transitions to balance energy efficiency and network responsiveness. In practical implementations, NB-IoT modules can be controlled through Python serial port libraries (e.g., pySerial) using AT commands, while MQTT/CoAP protocols facilitate cloud-based data interactions.

In an NB-IoT network, the UE, typically deployed as low-power IoT devices like smart meters or sensors, initiates communication by establishing a Radio Resource Control (RRC) connection with an enhanced NodeB (eNodeB) base station. This connection serves as the foundation for subsequent interactions with the EPC. Fig. 3, provides a general overview of the NB-IoT network.

A. Overview of NB-IoT Phy
1) Bandwidth:

The UL and DL of NB-IoT happen on different frequency bands. The UL of NB-IoT takes 180kHz bandwidth in total, and supports 15kHz or 3.75kHz subcarrier spacing. The DL of NB-IoT occupies 12 subcarries of 15kHz each, only supports a 15kHz subcarrier spacing [5].
2) Operation Modes:

NB-IoT is designed to coexist with LTE carriers without compromising LTE network performance. It achieves this through three primary deployment modes as summarized in Fig. 4:
Fig. 4. - Three operation modes.
Fig. 4.

Three operation modes.

∙ In-Band Mode: NB-IoT operates embedded within the LTE spectrum by occupying a single Physical Resource Block (PRB), ensuring resource-sharing compatibility with existing LTE infrastructure.

∙ Guard-Band Mode: Deployed within the unused guard bands of LTE carriers, this mode prevents interference with LTE’s primary frequency while utilizing otherwise wasted spectrum resources.

∙ Standalone Mode: Dedicated 180kHz bandwidth is allocated from legacy global system for mobile communication (GSM) systems’ 200kHz kHz carriers, with 10kHz guard bands on both sides to mitigate adjacent channel interference.
3) Framing:

The NB-IoT radio frame structure consists of 10 subframes (SFs) spanning a total duration of 10ms , maintaining temporal alignment with LTE specifications. As depicted in Figure 1, the DL frame format partitions each 1ms subframe into two equal 0.5ms slots, each containing 7 OFDM symbols with 15kHz subcarrier spacing. This configuration ensures backward compatibility with legacy LTE physical layer parameters while optimizing for narrowband operations.

For UL transmissions, the frame structure demonstrates subcarrier-dependent adaptability. When employing 15kHz spacing, the UL maintains identical slot and symbol configurations to the DL through Single Carrier Frequency Division Access (SC-FDMA) modulation. However, under 3.75kHz subcarrier spacing, a distinctive NB-IoT enhancement, the resource element (RE) mapping undergoes fundamental restructuring. Specifically, each 2ms time unit contains 7 SC-FDMA symbols, eliminating conventional subframe partitioning and enabling extended symbol durations for improved coverage. This adaptive resource grid configuration optimizes power efficiency in challenging propagation environments while maintaining spectral containment within the 180kHz channel bandwidth.
B. Signals and Channels

There are several kinds of signals and channels in the PHY of NB-IoT as defined in 3GPP Release 13 [4]. The summary of signals and channels of NB-IoT PHY is presented in Fig. 5. These channels and signals serve different purposes and employ various modulation methods. The details of each channels and signals in both DL and UL will be elaborated as following.

1) DL Case:

The DL in NB-IoT refers to the unidirectional communication path from the eNodeB to UE, which is fundamental for delivering control signaling and user data in cellular IoT systems. Operating within a narrow bandwidth of 180kHz , NB-IoT downlink leverages Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiple (OFDM) with a subcarrier spacing of 15kHz , ensuring compatibility with existing LTE infrastructure while optimizing spectral efficiency for low-power IoT applications. The physical layer adopts Quadrature Phase Shift Keying (QPSK) modulation, balancing robustness and energy efficiency in diverse channel conditions, particularly in deep-coverage scenarios. The signals and channels of NB-IoT DL are defined in [4] as:

∙ Narrowband Primary Synchronization Signal (NPSS). NPSS serves as a critical downlink synchronization reference in NB-IoT systems, UE to achieve time and frequency alignment with the serving cell. Defined in 3GPP TS 36.211 [31], the NPSS employs a Zadoff-Chu sequence of length 11 mapped to 11 contiguous subcarriers within the 180kHz bandwidth, transmitted across 11 consecutive OFDM symbols in subframe #5 of every radio frame. Unlike LTE PSS, the NPSS omits frequency-domain shifting to avoid interference in in-band deployment scenarios while maintaining backward compatibility. Its fixed subcarrier spacing of 15kHz and absence of cell-specific scrambling facilitate low-complexity UE implementations, crucial for energy-constrained IoT devices.

∙ Narrowband Secondary Synchronization Signal (NSSS). NSSS is a fundamental DL reference signal in NB-IoT systems, designed to facilitate frame timing synchronization and physical cell identity (PCI) detection. Also, defined in [31], the NSSS employs a length-131 Zadoff-Chu sequence modulated with a cell-specific cyclic shift and a binary phase shift keying (BPSK) scrambling sequence. This signal occupies 11 consecutive OFDM symbols across 12 subcarriers (180kHz bandwidth) in subframe #9 of every even-numbered radio frame, ensuring orthogonality with the NPSS in subframe #5 . The NSSS supports 504 unique PCIs through a combination of 126 root sequence indices and 4 cyclic shift values, enabling robust cell identification even under severe channel conditions.

∙ Narrowband Reference Signal (NRS). NRS is a critical demodulation reference for the DL of NB-IoT, enabling channel estimation for coherent detection of physical channels. The NRS employs frequency-domain Zadoff-Chu sequences with a configurable cyclic shift (0−5 ) and cell-specific scrambling derived from the PCI.

∙ Narrowband Physical Broadcast Channel (NPBCH). In NB-IoT, NPBCH is responsible for broadcasting critical system information to enable initial cell access and network synchronization. The NPBCH carries the Master Information Block (MIB) with a fixed Transport Block Size (TBS) of 34 bits, modulated using QPSK and protected by a 16-bit Cyclic Redundancy Check (CRC) for error detection. The channel employs a 640 ms transmission periodicity, distributed across 64 consecutive radio frames (64×10ms ) to support extended coverage through time diversity.

∙ Narrowband Physical Downlink Control Channel (NPDCCH). NPDCCH is the primary control channel in NB-IoT, responsible for transmitting Downlink Control Information (DCI) to UE for resource scheduling and system operation coordination. Defined in [31], the NPDCCH supports two DCI formats: Format N0 for UL grants and Format N1 for DL assignments, both modulated using QPSK with configurable code rates.

∙ Narrowband Physical Downlink Shared Channel (NPDSCH). NPDSCH functions as the primary data transport mechanism for NB-IoT in DL, responsible for delivering user-plane data and system information blocks (SIBs) to UE. The NPDSCH employs QPSK modulation with TBS ranging from 16 to 680 bits, configurable through 8-bit DCI in the NPDCCH.
2) UL Case:

In the NB-IoT architecture, the UL transmission refers to the communication path from user UE to the eNodeB, enabling data and control signaling delivery for IoT applications. The uplink design in NB-IoT is optimized for low-power, low-data-rate scenarios while ensuring extended coverage and massive connectivity. A fundamental characteristic of NB-IoT UL lies in its flexible resource allocation mechanism, which supports two subcarrier spacing options: 15 kHz and 3.75 kHz. The 15 kHz configuration maintains backward compatibility with legacy LTE systems, whereas the narrower 3.75 kHz subcarrier spacing enhances spectral efficiency and coverage by concentrating transmit power within a reduced bandwidth. This dual-mode approach allows dynamic adaptation to diverse IoT requirements, balancing transmission latency and energy consumption.

∙ Demodulation Reference Signal (DMRS). Actually, DMRS is widely used in wireless communication system. For NB-IoT, DMRS is a dedicated pilot signal integrated into the physical UL channels transmitting data or control commands.

∙ Narrowband Physical Random Access Channel (NPRACH). NPRACH is the random access channel in NB-IoT, designed to facilitate low-power, coverage-enhanced random access procedures for IoT devices initiating UL synchronization or connection requests. Defined in [31], NPRACH preambles consist of four symbol groups, each containing a CP and five identical symbols.

∙ Narrowband Physical Uplink Shared Channel (NPUSCH). NPUSCH serves as a fundamental UL transmission mechanism in NB-IoT, specifically engineered to support energy-efficient and coverage-optimized data transfer for IoT applications. NPUSCH is structured to accommodate two distinct transport formats: Format 1 for UL shared channel (UL-SCH) data transmission and Format 2 for hybrid automatic repeat request (HARQ) acknowledgment feedback. Unlike conventional LTE PUSCH, NPUSCH operates within a narrowband 180kHz bandwidth, utilizing either single-tone or multi-tone transmissions with configurable subcarrier spacing (3.75kHz or 15kHz ) to address varying coverage requirements and channel conditions. For single-tone operation, NPUSCH employs π2BPSK or π4QPSK with SC-FDMA modulation with low peak-to-average power ratio (PAPR), minimizing energy consumption for devices in extreme coverage scenarios (e.g., deep indoor or underground deployments). Multi-tone configurations, supporting up to 12 subcarriers, enhance spectral efficiency for latency-tolerant applications in moderate coverage conditions.

Critical components in this process are the NPSS for time-frequency synchronization, NSSS for Cell-ID detection, NPBCH for MIB acquisition, and NPDCCH for resource allocation signaling. The DL reception initiates with NPSS-based synchronization, where auto-correlation techniques are employed to estimate TO and compensate for CFO. Subsequent to achieving symbol-level alignment, the received waveform undergoes subframe boundary detection using NPSS’s known position in subframe #5 of every radio frame. With established time-frequency synchronization and cell-specific parameters, the receiver proceeds to demodulate NPDCCH and NPUSCH using QPSK constellation mapping.

1) Time-Domain Synchronization:

Time-domain synchronization refers to the process of detecting the position of the NPSS within the received waveform to establish a timing reference for subframe alignment [32], [33]. In NB-IoT systems, the NPSS exhibits a deterministic time-domain structure with a fixed repetition interval, enabling robust detection even under severe coverage conditions. Synchronization accuracy hinges critically on exploiting the inherent properties of NPSS. Maximum-likelihood (ML) estimation emerges as the preferred approach owing to its asymptotic efficiency in synchronization tasks [34], [35], [36]. While both auto-correlation [37] and cross-correlation methodologies are theoretically viable for NPSS detection, the cross-correlation [38] technique, despite being ML-optimal, imposes impractical computational complexity and requires a priori knowledge of the reference sequence. Consequently, we employ auto-correlation-based detection, capitalizing on the intrinsic self-correlation property of ZC sequences that exhibits robustness against frequency/phase offsets.

C. CFO Estimation and Compensation

In SDR communications, CFO is often more pronounced than in dedicated hardware. This is primarily due to the discrete nature of sampling frequencies in SDRs. For instance, the USRP N210, with a main clock of 100 MHz, derives its sampling rate by integer division (e.g., 100MHz/52≈1.923077MHz ), preventing an exact match to the standard 1.92 MHz rate. While devices like the USRP B210 (with a 61.44 MHz clock) can achieve exact rates, others such as RTL-SDR dongles and the HackRF One face similar constraints. Furthermore, voltage and temperature variations exacerbate sampling clock instability, collectively making CFO a particularly severe issue in SDR-based systems. CFO estimation is performed by exploiting the time-domain structure of the NPSS, which provides phase coherence across its repeated sequences. Following initial time-domain synchronization, the CFO is quantified through cross-correlation analysis between the received NPSS waveform and its locally generated reference. The phase rotation observed over the NPSS subframes is used to derive a coarse CFO estimate via LS algorithms. Given that the NPSS is transmitted in the subframe #5 , which is located in the middle of NB-IoT DL frame, NPSS is a good representation of the CFO of the whole frame [32].

D. NPBCH, NPDCCH, NPDSCH Demodulation

As the core elements of NB-IoT downlink communication, the NPBCH, NPDCCH, NPDSCH collectively enable critical functionalities such as system information dissemination, resource scheduling, and user data transmission. Demodulating these channels requires meticulous handling of their unique signal structures, modulation schemes, and protocol-specific constraints.

Following the initial synchronization and frequency offset correction derived from the NPSS, the downlink signal is segmented into its constituent physical channels (NPBCH, NPDCCH, and NPDSCH) based on the synchronized timing hierarchy. Leveraging the NRS embedded within each subframe, channel estimation is performed to characterize the multipath propagation and residual phase errors inherent in NB-IoT’s low-complexity transceivers. This subframe-level processing ensures robust symbol recovery despite severe fading and ultra-low SNR conditions, adhering to the repetition and scrambling mechanism. The demodulated outputs are then forwarded to higher-layer protocols for further decoding and system operation.
1) CP Remove:

CP refers to a prefix of a symbol with a duplicate ending in an OFDM wireless system. The receiver is typically configured to drop a cyclic prefix sample CP that can be used to counteract the effects of multipath propagation. In NB-IoT systems, we use CP to eliminate inter-symbol interference. According to the design of NB-IoT PHY, the length symbol varies from 137−138 samples, with the length of CP varies from 9−10 samples. Therefore, we need to remove the CP for each symbol and retain the 128-sample-length symbol. For the first symbol in a slot, the CP occupies 10 samples and occupies 9 samples for the rest 6 symbols in the slot.

The NB-IoT UL physical layer introduces distinct design paradigms compared to the downlink, primarily due to its support for both single-tone and multi-tone transmissions. Baseband Signal Generation Differences: The physical layer processing chains differ fundamentally between transmission modes in three key aspects:

Single-Tone Signal Path:

    Symbol Modulation: For single-tone mode, π/2 -BPSK and π/4 -QPSK are both optional for symbol modulation.

    Phase Rotation: After initial symbol mapping, all constellation points undergo specific phase rotations. The rotation sequence is defined in [31]. The phase rotation applied in NPUSCH single-tone transmission transforms the detrimental, continuous phase drift induced by residual CFO into a deterministic, periodic variation. This enables simplified and more robust receiver processing, as the known rotation can be easily compensated, effectively mitigating inter-symbol interference and maintaining link reliability under significant frequency errors.

    Direct Subcarrier Mapping: The rotated symbols are directly mapped to a single designated subcarrier without any additional transformation processing. This single-subcarrier approach maintains constant-envelope characteristics throughout transmission.

    Orthogonal Subcarrier Modulation: The modulated subcarrier undergoes specialized Orthogonal Subcarrier Modulation to generate the final time-domain waveform. This simplified processing chain enables ultra-low latency transmission suitable for deep coverage applications.

Multi-Tone Signal Path:

    Symbol Modulation: For multi-tone case, only QPSK is optional for symbol modulation.

    Subcarrier Allocation: After initial symbol mapping, data symbols are allocated to multiple subcarriers (3, 6, or 12) according to scheduled resource assignments. This multi-subcarrier approach enables higher data rates but requires complex resource mapping.

    FFT Precoding: Symbols undergo FFT precoding, converting time-domain symbols to frequency-domain representations. This transform precoding stage is essential for SC-FDMA operation and maintains low PAPR characteristics.

    IFFT Processing: The precoded symbols are mapped to physical subcarriers within the 180 kHz bandwidth, then processed through an Inverse Fast Fourier Transform (IFFT) to generate the final time-domain waveform. This multi-stage processing enables frequency-domain equalization at the receiver.

Key Implementation Differences:

    Phase Handling: Single-tone mandates phase rotation before transmission while multi-tone implements phase correction during reception

    Processing Complexity: Single-tone uses direct modulation requiring minimal processing; multi-tone requires FFT + IFFT stages

    Resource Allocation: Single-tone uses fixed single-subcarrier mapping; multi-tone supports flexible subcarrier bundling (3/6/12 )

    Latency Profile: Single-tone processing has significantly lower latency due to its simplified signal path

    Receiver Requirements: Single-tone demands specialized phase tracking loops while multi-tone uses conventional frequency-domain equalization

    For wireless signal synchronization, reference signal (e.g., DMRS for NB-IoT) is always a useful pattern [49]. However, as DMRS for single-tone case is sine wave with a given initial phase, it’s hard to achieve synchronization based on DMRS for single-tone.

    CFO compensation:

    Due to moderate instability in SDR equipment, the frequency offset estimated during NPRACH often differs significantly from those observed in previous NPUSCH transmissions. Furthermore, frequency offset varies across different NPUSCH transmission instances. Consequently, we must perform separate frequency offset estimation for each individual NPUSCH transmission [50].

    In single-carrier systems, CFO arises from oscillator mismatches and Doppler effects during transmission. The normalized CFO is defined as ϵ=foΔf , where fo=fc−f′c denotes the absolute frequency offset between transmitter (fc ) and receiver (f′c ), with Δf being the sub-carrier spacing. This offset induces progressive phase rotation during demodulation, causing constellation distortion and BER degradation.

    ϵ^=12π(LCP+N)arg{∑n=1LCP+Ny∗[n]x[n]},(35)

∙ Configurable BPF:

In multi-tone NPUSCH reception, the bandpass filtering stage features dynamic bandwidth scaling directly determined by the active subcarrier configuration, with operational bandwidths of 65 kHz, 110 kHz, or 200 kHz corresponding respectively to 3, 6, and 12 active subcarrier deployments. This adaptive bandwidth strategy comprehensively envelopes the entire transmission band while maintaining critical guard bands, effectively containing the spread spectrum energy within licensed frequency allocations and preventing detrimental spectral leakage into adjacent channels. The precisely calibrated bandwidth margins—specifically, ±32.5 kHz for 3-subcarriers, ±55 kHz for 6-subcarriers, and ±100 kHz for 12-subcarriers configurations—deliver dual technical advantages: firstly, robust immunity against ICI caused by oscillator phase noise; secondly, intrinsic accommodation of worst-case frequency offsets up to ±20 ppm (±1.8 kHz at 900 MHz) without requiring preliminary frequency correction in the signal acquisition phase. By maintaining strict conformity to the instantaneous transmission bandwidth rather than fixing to the full system bandwidth, this implementation achieves an optimal balance between adjacent channel rejection and in-band signal preservation, directly enhancing SNR across typical urban fading scenarios while simultaneously reducing UE power consumption through elimination of over-filtering effects.

∙ Synchronization and CFO compensation:

This complexity arises as they essentially represent the superposition of multiple sine waves with distinct initial phase offsets. Consequently, synchronization algorithms leveraging maximum correlation peaks generally demonstrate enhanced effectiveness in multi-tone case. Channel Modeling and Simulation

NB-IoT systems experience significant signal degradation in urban and industrial environments due to multipath fading and frequency offsets. To accurately simulate these challenging conditions, we employs Rayleigh fading channel models as the primary framework. This selection is motivated by NB-IoT’s typical operation in non-line-of-sight (NLoS) scenarios, where signals undergo diffuse reflections from buildings, machinery, and terrain—precisely the conditions where Rayleigh fading effectively characterizes the rapid amplitude fluctuations resulting from destructive interference of multipath components.
A. Rayleigh Fading Fundamentals

The Rayleigh fading model assumes a large number of scattered signal paths with no dominant line-of-sight component. The probability density function for the received signal amplitude r is given by:
f(r)=rσ2e−r2/(2σ2),r≥0,(48)

Through the practical implementation and validation of our SDR-based NB-IoT physical layer system across diverse commercial platforms and real-world environments, we have distilled several critical insights that extend beyond theoretical specifications. These lessons provide valuable guidance for researchers and engineers undertaking similar software-defined wireless system developments.

    Hardware Selection Directly Impacts Algorithmic Complexity. The choice of SDR platform significantly influences signal processing requirements. We observed substantial variations in oscillator stability and sampling rate accuracy across different COTS devices. This variation stems from the fundamental clock architecture of the devices. The sampling rate is typically derived from a high-speed main clock (e.g., 61.44 MHz, 100 MHz) via an integer or fractional frequency divider. For instance, while the USRP B210’s 61.44 MHz main clock enables exact division to the standard 1.92 MHz sampling rate, platforms like the USRP N210 (100 MHz clock) and HackRF One require fractional division, resulting in non-standard rates (approximately 1.923077 MHz for N210). This necessitates careful hardware-aware design, including potential resampling operations and more robust frequency offset compensation algorithms. The practical implication is that algorithm development cannot be divorced from target hardware characteristics, researchers must consult device datasheets thoroughly and implement adaptive processing chains that account for platform-specific limitations. In our design, we use CFO compensation method (e.g., Costas Loop, NRS-based CFO compensation) to mitigate errors cansed by the variations of SDR device.

    Environmental Conditions Dictate Practical Deployment Strategies. Our empirical validation revealed dramatic performance differences between LOS and NLOS scenarios. The significant degradation in NLOS conditions, characterized by severe multipath fading and attenuation, underscores that real-world deployments cannot assume ideal propagation environments. The consequence of relying on higher repetition counts in NLOS scenarios is increased energy consumption, which directly impacts UE battery lifetime. To mitigate this, deployment strategies should prioritize optimal antenna placement and selection. Additionally, careful configuration of power saving modes and repetition parameters is essential to balance reliability and energy efficiency. This experience emphasizes that practical system design must prioritize robustness over optimal performance. For reliable operation, deployment planning should favor LOS configurations where possible, while for unavoidable NLOS scenarios, strategies such as signal amplification, antenna diversity, or leveraging NB-IoT’s coverage enhancement mechanisms with higher repetition counts become essential rather than optional.

    Frequency Offset Management Requires Continuous Adaptation. A critical lesson from our implementation is that CFO constitutes an ongoing challenge rather than a one-time calibration issue. Initial coarse compensation during synchronization proved insufficient due to residual offsets from temperature-dependent oscillator drift and Doppler effects. This necessitated implementing a hierarchical frequency recovery approach combining initial estimation with continuous tracking mechanisms. The insight is that effective CFO mitigation requires treating frequency alignment as a dynamic process throughout the communication session, particularly important for maintaining link stability in mobile scenarios or with low-cost hardware exhibiting significant phase noise.

These lessons highlight the essential interplay between hardware constraints, environmental factors, and algorithmic robustness—insights that emerge only through hands-on implementation and comprehensive validation across diverse operational conditions. They underscore the importance of adopting a systems-level perspective that integrates theoretical models with practical realities when developing SDR-based communication platforms.

SECTION VIII.
Conclusion

This comprehensive tutorial has established a foundational framework for SDR-based NB-IoT physical layer implementation, bridging critical gaps between 3GPP specifications and real-world deployments. Through systematic exploration of synchronization robustness, channel estimation accuracy, and performance validation methodologies, we have demonstrated a fully functional end-to-end PHY implementation supporting both single-tone and multi-tone transmissions across diverse COTS SDR platforms. The novel signal processing pipeline incorporating NPSS/CP-based synchronization, CFO compensation, and adaptive channel equalization shows reliability in microbenchmarks.

Experimental validation across indoor/outdoor environments confirms the system’s practical viability, with HackRF One and USRP platforms demonstrating consistent demodulation performance while maintaining 3GPP compliance. The implementation’s flexibility facilitates rapid prototyping of emerging techniques, particularly AI-enhanced demodulation and integrated sensing-communication paradigms that leverage the deterministic structure of NPUSCH waveforms.

Beyond immediate technical contributions, this tutorial establishes a reproducible experimental foundation for next-generation NB-IoT research. The open architecture enables validation of advanced 3GPP protocols. By harmonizing theoretical rigor with practical SDR implementation insights, this work accelerates innovation in low-power wide-area communications for smart infrastructure and industrial IoT applications.
